Executive Summary

ClearScore AI Ltd presents as a technically mature vendor with structured documentation and baseline compliance alignment. However, assurance is heavily dependent on vendor-provided representations, with limited independent validation of model behaviour under real-world conditions.

The primary risk is not a lack of controls — it is over-reliance on opaque processes. Particularly in model training, bias mitigation, and drift handling, Veriflow would be accepting the vendor's self-assessment rather than independently verifiable evidence. This is common in the AI vendor market. It is still a risk that requires active management.

Arken Systems recommends provisional approval with restrictions, subject to the contractual safeguards and monitoring obligations set out in this document. This system should be treated as continuously at risk of degradation, not as a stable control once deployed.

01

Scope of Assessment

AI system assessed: Credit scoring model used for lending decisions within Veriflow's merchant onboarding and credit product workflows.

Roles under EU AI Act: ClearScore AI Ltd is the provider (responsible for model development, training, and technical documentation under Article 11). Veriflow Payments Ltd is the deployer (responsible for use-context risk management, FRIA, human oversight, and post-market monitoring under Articles 26–27).

Regulatory context governing this assessment: EU AI Act (high-risk system, Annex III — credit and financial services); UK GDPR Article 22 (automated decision-making and profiling); FCA Consumer Duty (consumer fairness and explainability expectations); Equality Act 2010 (non-discrimination in lending decisions).

This document focuses on Veriflow's deployer-side due diligence. It does not substitute for ClearScore AI's provider-side technical documentation obligations, which are a separate contractual and regulatory matter.

03

Where Assurance Breaks Down

For each claim area, this section identifies what the vendor has not provided, what independent validation is absent, and where the residual risk sits. These are not hypothetical concerns — they are gaps in the evidence base that would prevent a confident approval without conditions.

3.1 Training Data Transparency High Risk

ClearScore AI provides high-level descriptions of training data sources but does not disclose dataset composition in sufficient detail to assess representativeness against Veriflow's actual applicant population.

  • No visibility into demographic distribution of training data
  • No disclosure of historical bias that may be embedded in labelled outcomes
  • Performance validation conducted against vendor-selected test sets, not Veriflow's population
Bias in a credit model cannot be independently verified if the training data is opaque. This is a common position in the vendor market — most AI vendors treat training data as proprietary. Common does not mean acceptable. The contractual requirement should be for ongoing demographic performance reporting, not access to the underlying data.
3.2 Bias and Fairness Claims High Risk

Fairness testing is described in vendor documentation, but the methodology is not fully disclosed. Veriflow cannot independently assess whether the fairness definition used is appropriate for its regulatory context.

  • No clarity on which fairness metric was optimised (demographic parity, equalised odds, calibration) — these produce different outcomes and involve explicit trade-offs
  • Accepted thresholds for acceptable disparity not disclosed
  • Post-deployment bias monitoring is vendor-controlled with no independent validation layer
The vendor's fairness documentation looks reasonable on paper. What it doesn't tell us is what they chose to optimise for — and therefore what they chose not to optimise for. A model can be "fair" on one metric and discriminatory on another. Veriflow needs to know which trade-offs were made before it can assess whether those trade-offs are acceptable under FCA Consumer Duty and the Equality Act.
3.3 Model Explainability Medium-High Risk

ClearScore AI provides feature importance outputs — the factors that contributed most to a given decision. This is partial, not full, explainability.

  • Explanations are post-hoc approximations of model behaviour, not direct traces through the model's decision logic
  • Feature importance outputs are not always meaningful to applicants or to frontline staff handling disputes
  • No testing of whether explanations provided are accurate representations of actual decision factors
"Explainable" does not mean understandable. Feature importance scores ("your application was affected by: payment history, credit utilisation, account age") satisfy a surface-level explanation obligation but may not meet the meaningful explanation standard implied by UK GDPR Article 22 or FCA Consumer Duty in dispute situations. Veriflow should not assume the vendor's explanation output discharges its own explanation obligations to affected customers.
3.4 Model Drift and Monitoring High Risk

Production monitoring exists, but it is entirely vendor-controlled. Veriflow has no independent visibility into model performance degradation in its own deployment context.

  • No deployer-side monitoring layer — Veriflow is dependent on the vendor to identify and report drift
  • No defined thresholds at which Veriflow would be notified of performance degradation
  • No defined criteria for retraining — decision sits entirely with the vendor
  • No contractual obligation for the vendor to notify Veriflow before a model update is deployed
This is where AI systems fail in practice. Not in a dramatic incident, but through gradual degradation that nobody notices because nobody is watching from the deployer's side. If the model quietly becomes less accurate or more biased over time, Veriflow would not know until it manifested as a regulatory finding or customer complaint. An independent monitoring layer is not optional — it is a basic deployer responsibility under EU AI Act Article 26.
3.5 Third-Party and Sub-Processor Risk Medium-High Risk

Limited visibility into ClearScore AI's upstream dependencies — data providers, infrastructure, and any sub-processors involved in model training or inference.

  • No sub-processor disclosure provided in contract draft
  • Cloud infrastructure provider not identified — relevant for data residency and resilience obligations
  • No contractual notification requirement if ClearScore AI changes a material sub-processor
Fourth-party risk receives less attention than it deserves. If ClearScore AI relies on a third-party data enrichment provider that has a breach, or changes its underlying model infrastructure, Veriflow may have no visibility and no contractual protection. This is a relatively modest gap to close contractually — it just needs to be asked for explicitly.
3.6 Security and Baseline Controls Medium Risk

ClearScore AI holds ISO 27001 certification. This is the lowest-risk area of this assessment. Standard security due diligence applies — certificate validity, scope coverage, and penetration testing cadence should be confirmed.

ISO 27001 certification provides reasonable assurance of baseline security controls. It does not provide assurance about AI-specific risks. Do not conflate the two.
04

Due Diligence Questions — What We Would Ask

These questions are not generic security questionnaire items. They are targeted at the specific pressure points identified in Section 3. A vendor that cannot answer these questions concretely should not be approved.

Bias and Fairness

  • Which fairness metric did you optimise for during training, and why did you choose that metric over alternatives?
  • What disparity thresholds did you set as acceptable, and what was the rationale for those thresholds?
  • What trade-offs did you accept — where did optimising for one fairness property create worse outcomes on another?
  • Can you show us a case where the model performed poorly against a demographic group, and what you did about it?
  • How is bias monitored in production, and what would trigger an investigation?

Monitoring and Drift

  • What performance metrics do you monitor in production, and at what thresholds would you notify us of degradation?
  • Who decides when the model needs retraining, and what is the process?
  • Will we receive notification before a model update is deployed to our environment?
  • Can we suspend or override model decisions independently if we identify a problem?
  • What is the rollback process if a model update causes an adverse outcome?

Explainability

  • Can you provide decision-level explanations adequate for a customer who has been declined credit to understand the primary reasons?
  • Have you tested whether the explanations you provide accurately reflect the model's actual decision logic, or are they post-hoc approximations?
  • How do you handle disputes where a customer challenges the explanation provided?

Incident Handling

  • How do you define an AI incident — specifically, what would and would not trigger your incident response process?
  • What is your notification timeline to clients in the event of a model-related incident?
  • Have you had an AI incident with a client in the past two years? If so, what happened and how was it resolved?

Sub-Processors and Infrastructure

  • Who are your material sub-processors, and what is the contractual mechanism if you need to change one?
  • Where is data processed during inference, and does this meet our data residency requirements?

Required Controls

The following controls are required as conditions of approval. They are not recommendations. Approval is contingent on the Before Approval items being addressed contractually prior to go-live.

Before Approval — Contractual Requirements

  • Bias reporting obligationContractual requirement for quarterly disaggregated performance reporting by demographic group, using metrics agreed with Veriflow. Vendor cannot self-select which metrics to report.
  • Incident notification timelineAI incidents affecting Veriflow's deployment must be notified within 72 hours of identification. Contract must define what constitutes an AI incident.
  • Pre-deployment notification for model updatesVendor must provide minimum 10 business days' notice before deploying any model update to Veriflow's environment. Veriflow retains the right to defer updates pending review.
  • Sub-processor disclosure and change notificationComplete sub-processor list to be provided and maintained. Any material change to sub-processors requires prior notification and Veriflow's consent where data processing is affected.
  • Audit rightsContract to include a right to audit or commission a third-party audit of model performance and fairness controls, exercisable annually and following any AI incident.
  • Suspension and override rightsVeriflow must have the contractual and technical ability to suspend model-assisted decisions and revert to manual processing independently of the vendor.

After Deployment — Veriflow Internal Controls

  • Independent monitoring layerVeriflow to implement its own production monitoring of decision outcomes — approval rates, decline rates, demographic disparity — independent of vendor reporting. This is a deployer obligation under EU AI Act Article 26, not a vendor service.
  • Defined alert thresholdsInternal thresholds to be set for approval rate changes, demographic disparity indicators, and explanation quality. Breaching a threshold triggers a formal review, not just escalation to the vendor.
  • Human oversight protocolDefined categories of decisions that require human review regardless of model confidence score. Procedure for handling cases where human reviewer disagrees with model output.

Ongoing — Review and Assurance Cycle

  • Quarterly vendor reviewFormal review of vendor-provided performance reports, bias metrics, and any incidents or near-misses since last review.
  • Annual independent re-validationCommission independent assessment of model fairness and performance against Veriflow's actual applicant population. Not reliant on vendor data.
  • Trigger-based reassessmentFull due diligence reassessment triggered by: any AI incident, any model version change, any material change to Veriflow's applicant population or product scope, or any new regulatory guidance affecting credit scoring AI.
07

Conditions Under Which We Would Not Onboard This Vendor

Provisional approval is contingent on the conditions in Section 6 being met. The following circumstances would change the recommendation to rejection, regardless of other factors.

Rejection Conditions

  • The vendor refuses to provide contractual notification obligations for model updates or AI incidents — accepting vendor assurance without contractual commitment is not a governance control
  • Disaggregated bias metrics reveal material disparate impact against a protected characteristic group and the vendor cannot provide a credible remediation plan with defined timelines
  • The vendor has experienced an undisclosed AI incident affecting another deployer in a credit or financial services context
  • Audit rights are refused or qualified to the point of being non-exercisable in practice
  • Veriflow cannot implement an independent suspension capability — if we cannot turn this off without the vendor's cooperation, we cannot manage the risk
  • Sub-processor disclosure reveals a data processing arrangement that is incompatible with Veriflow's data residency or UK GDPR obligations
08

Final Decision and Rationale

Assessment Conclusion
Provisionally approved with restrictions

The capability ClearScore AI provides is valuable and the risks identified are manageable — provided they are actively managed. The risks are not acceptable if Veriflow treats this as a standard SaaS procurement and assumes the vendor's assurance documentation is sufficient.

The approval is provisional because it depends on contractual conditions that have not yet been confirmed. If the vendor does not accept the notification, audit, and oversight requirements set out in Section 6, the risk profile changes materially and the recommendation changes with it.

The single most important principle governing this deployment: this system should be treated as continuously at risk of degradation, not as a stable control. AI models change. Applicant populations change. Regulatory expectations change. A due diligence assessment conducted at onboarding is not a permanent clearance — it is the beginning of an ongoing governance obligation.
