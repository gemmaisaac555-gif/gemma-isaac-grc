## The Timeline, Honestly Read

The EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024. Its obligations apply in phases. The headline dates are well-documented. What is less often discussed is what those dates actually mean in practice for a financial services firm that is deploying AI systems today.

**February 2025 — Prohibited practices**

Obligations relating to prohibited AI practices applied from 2 February 2025. These include AI systems that manipulate individuals through subliminal techniques, exploit vulnerabilities of specific groups, and certain uses of biometric categorisation and emotion recognition. For most financial services firms, these prohibitions do not directly affect current operations. But they set the tone. The Act draws hard lines, and firms should know where those lines are before their product teams find them by accident.

**August 2025 — GPAI model obligations**

Obligations for providers of general-purpose AI models apply from 2 August 2025. This is primarily relevant to AI developers, not deployers. That said, firms integrating these models into their own systems should understand what their providers are now required to do, and whether those requirements show up in contracts. In practice, they often don't.

**August 2026 — High-risk AI system obligations**

The obligations that apply to financial services deployers come into force on 2 August 2026. This includes credit scoring, insurance risk assessment, employment screening, and certain types of customer-facing decision-making. Seventeen months. That sounds like a long time. It is not, if you are starting from scratch.

**August 2027 — Legacy systems**

High-risk AI systems already in use before August 2026 have an additional 12 months to comply. This looks like runway. It is not. It creates a decision point: which systems you continue under transition, and which you bring into compliance early. Most firms take longer than they should to make this call.

## What Financial Services Firms Are Getting Wrong

**1. Treating August 2026 as the start date rather than the deadline**

The most common mistake is reading the compliance date as the point to begin. It is not. For a high-risk AI system, full compliance requires: risk management, data governance, technical documentation, FRIA, human oversight, testing, monitoring, and registration. These are not checklist items. They are operational capabilities. If you are deploying a high-risk AI system and have not started your compliance programme, you are already late.

**2. Assuming the FCA will interpret the Act for them**

UK firms face a structural ambiguity. The EU AI Act does not apply domestically, but FCA expectations are developing alongside it. The direction is clear. The FCA is not going to ignore the EU AI Act — it is going to absorb its principles and apply them through supervision. Firms that align with EU AI Act standards are unlikely to fall short of FCA expectations. Firms that do neither are taking a position they would struggle to defend in front of a regulator.

**3. Treating compliance as a legal function rather than a governance function**

EU AI Act compliance is not a legal sign-off exercise. It requires input from data science, risk, operations, HR, and business owners. A legal team cannot run a FRIA in isolation. A compliance team cannot design a working oversight model alone. Firms that treat this as a documentation exercise will produce documentation. They will not produce governance. There is a difference, and regulators are getting better at spotting it.

**4. Underestimating the deployer obligations**

The provider/deployer split is widely misunderstood. The burden falls on the deployer. Buying a system does not transfer that burden.

**5. Conflating the transition period with regulatory latitude**

The transition period is a timing extension, not a standards reduction. It offers a strategic option. Most companies are deferring that decision. That deferral is a risk in itself.

## What Prudent Organisations Are Doing Now

### Now — Foundation work

- **AI system inventory** — you cannot control what you cannot see. Most companies still lack a full inventory.
- **Deployer obligation mapping** — understand what the Act specifically requires of you as a deployer, not the provider.
- **Provider contract review** — check whether your provider contracts reflect EU AI Act obligations. In practice, they often do not.

### By Q3 2025 — Governance infrastructure

- **Human oversight framework** — often approached as a documentation task. It is not. It needs to work in practice.
- **FRIA process** — the assessment itself, and the process for conducting it going forward.
- **AI literacy training** — required under EU AI Act Art. 26(6). Frequently overlooked.

### By Q1 2026 — Compliance readiness

- **Post-market monitoring plan** — articulated and operational, not described in a policy that does not reflect reality.
- **Registration preparation** — begin assembling the information required for the EU AI Act database.
- **Compliance testing** — internal assessment of whether governance is substantive and functional. This is where reality bites. Either the governance works, or it does not.

---

## The Regulatory Intuition

The intuition is simple. The EU AI Act is not really about the documentation. It is about whether the governance is real. The documentation is a means of demonstrating that governance — to the regulator, to affected parties, and to the organisation itself. Companies with functioning governance will find compliance straightforward. Companies without will find that documentation cannot compensate for operational deficiencies.

## Implications for Veriflow Payments

Veriflow has identified its key high-risk AI system — the ClearScore credit scoring model. Some of the basics are in place. Not all.

- **Inventory:** Partially complete. Should be verified across all teams.
- **Deployer obligation mapping:** Underway. Not completed.
- **FRIA:** Conducted retrospectively, with some identified shortcomings.
- **Human oversight:** Only partially in place.
- **AI literacy training:** Still to be provided.
- **Monitoring:** Present, but not fully articulated into a monitoring plan.
- **Registration preparation:** Still to be initiated.

**Overall position:** stronger than many firms at a similar point, but still reliant on effective execution.

---

## Overall Assessment

The distance between current state and August 2026 compliance is bridgeable. The major challenge is not regulatory technicality. It is execution. More precisely, it is the difference between knowing what is needed and actually delivering it. That is the chasm across which most governance initiatives fall. It is also where most regulators ultimately focus.
