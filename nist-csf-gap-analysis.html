Executive Summary

Arken Systems was engaged by Veriflow Payments Ltd to conduct a structured gap analysis against the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0. This assessment evaluates Veriflow's current cybersecurity posture across all six CSF 2.0 Functions — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover — and produces a prioritised remediation roadmap aligned to Veriflow's operational context as a regulated payment services provider operating under FCA supervision and PCI-DSS obligations.

Veriflow processes approximately £2.4 billion in annual transaction volume across open banking, B2B payment rails, and embedded finance integrations. Its threat surface is materially shaped by third-party API dependency, real-time transaction data flows, and a growing AI-assisted fraud detection capability — the latter introducing emerging obligations under the EU AI Act applicable to its EU-facing operations.

Overall maturity rating: Developing (Tier 2). Veriflow demonstrates risk-aware practices in several areas but lacks consistent formalisation, executive-level cybersecurity accountability structures, and systematic supply chain risk management. The Govern and Respond functions present the most acute gaps and are assessed at Tier 1.

Tier Definitions

  • Tier 1 — PartialAd hoc, largely reactive practices with limited organisational awareness of cybersecurity risk.
  • Tier 2 — Risk InformedPractices exist but are not organisation-wide, formally approved, or consistently implemented.
  • Tier 3 — RepeatableFormally approved, consistently implemented, and communicated across the organisation.
  • Tier 4 — AdaptiveActively adapts based on lessons learned, threat intelligence, and continuous improvement processes.

Target state for this engagement is Tier 3 across all functions, representing a credible and auditable baseline appropriate for a regulated payment services provider. The Recover function targets Tier 2 — see rationale in the Analyst Notes section.

01

Govern

T1 → T3  ·  🔴 High Gap

What CSF 2.0 Requires

The Govern function — new in CSF 2.0 — establishes that cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy are understood at the organisational level and integrated into enterprise risk management. It covers organisational context, risk management strategy, roles and responsibilities, policy, oversight, and supply chain risk management.

Current State

Veriflow has an information security policy (last reviewed 18 months ago) and a designated CISO-equivalent responsibility held by the Head of Technology — a dual role with no dedicated security resource beneath it. There is no documented cybersecurity risk appetite statement approved by the board. Cybersecurity is not a standing agenda item at board level. Third-party risk is managed informally through procurement, with no standardised security questionnaire process or tiered vendor classification.

Identified Gaps

  • GV.OC — Organisational ContextRegulatory obligations (FCA, PCI-DSS, UK GDPR, EU AI Act) have not been formally mapped to the cybersecurity programme. Business context — including critical assets, regulatory scope, and stakeholder obligations — is held implicitly rather than documented.
  • GV.RM — Risk Management StrategyNo board-approved cybersecurity risk appetite statement exists. Risk tolerance decisions are made operationally without executive mandate. There is no defined risk register update cadence.
  • GV.RR — Roles, Responsibilities, AuthoritiesThe CISO function is under-resourced and structurally combined with technology delivery leadership, creating a potential conflict of objectives. Cybersecurity responsibilities for non-technical staff are undefined.
  • GV.PO — PolicyThe information security policy has not been reviewed since 2024. There is no AI governance policy despite Veriflow's deployment of an AI-assisted fraud detection model.
  • GV.OV — OversightNo board or audit committee mechanism exists for receiving and acting on cybersecurity risk reports. There is no KRI/KPI framework for cybersecurity.
  • GV.SC — Supply Chain Risk ManagementApproximately 40% of Veriflow's critical payment processing functions rely on third-party APIs or cloud services with no formal security assurance process beyond initial due diligence.

02

Identify

T2 → T3  ·  🟡 Medium Gap

Current State

Veriflow maintains an informal asset inventory managed within its CMDB, but this has not been validated against actual infrastructure in over 12 months. A risk assessment was conducted in 2024 as part of a PCI-DSS audit cycle but has not been updated to reflect a significant cloud migration completed in late 2025. There is no formal business impact analysis beyond the continuity plan documentation.

Identified Gaps

  • ID.AM — Asset ManagementThe CMDB does not capture data flows between internal systems and third-party APIs. SaaS tools adopted by business teams outside IT procurement are not systematically recorded. The AI fraud detection model is not documented as a critical asset.
  • ID.RA — Risk AssessmentThe most recent risk assessment predates the cloud migration and the deployment of the AI fraud model. There is no process for triggering risk assessment updates following material changes to the technology environment.
  • ID.IM — ImprovementNo formal lessons-learned process feeds back into the risk identification cycle. Near-miss events are not systematically recorded.

03

Protect

T2 → T3  ·  🟡 Medium Gap

Current State

Veriflow has implemented core protective controls including MFA for all staff, role-based access control, endpoint protection, and encryption of data at rest and in transit consistent with PCI-DSS requirements. These controls are operational but are not formally documented as a controls library, making audit readiness labour-intensive. Security awareness training is delivered annually but is not role-differentiated.

Identified Gaps

  • PR.AA — Identity Management and Access ControlPrivileged access review is conducted inconsistently — most recently 14 months ago. There is no automated process for revoking access upon role change or departure; this is managed through a manual HR-IT workflow with documented delays.
  • PR.AT — Awareness and TrainingAnnual training is generic. High-risk roles (finance, API integration teams, customer operations) do not receive targeted threat scenario training relevant to payment fraud, social engineering, or API abuse.
  • PR.DS — Data SecurityData classification policy exists but is not operationalised — staff do not consistently apply classification labels, and there is no tooling enforcing handling rules.
  • PR.PS — Platform SecurityPatch management SLAs exist for critical vulnerabilities (30 days) but compliance against this SLA is not tracked or reported. Shadow IT represents an unmanaged exposure.

Remediation Recommendations

04

Detect

T2 → T3  ·  🟡 Medium Gap

Current State

Veriflow operates Microsoft Sentinel deployed 18 months ago. Alert tuning is ongoing and the current false positive rate is high, creating analyst fatigue. There is no dedicated SOC function; monitoring is performed by two IT engineers on a business-hours basis. There is no detection coverage outside business hours.

Identified Gaps

  • DE.CM — Continuous MonitoringOut-of-hours coverage is absent. There is no process for detecting anomalous behaviour in the AI fraud detection model's outputs — model drift or adversarial manipulation would not be detected under current arrangements.
  • DE.AE — Adverse Event AnalysisAlert triage is informal and undocumented. There are no defined escalation thresholds. Correlation rules in Sentinel have not been reviewed against the current threat landscape since initial deployment.

Remediation Recommendations

05

Respond

T1 → T3  ·  🔴 High Gap

Current State

Veriflow has a basic incident response runbook, last updated in 2023, covering generic IT incidents and data breaches. It does not address payment fraud scenarios, API compromise, ransomware, or supply chain incidents. The runbook has never been tested. Regulatory notification obligations (FCA, ICO 72-hour GDPR deadline) are referenced but not procedurally mapped to specific staff actions.

Identified Gaps

  • RS.MA — Incident ManagementThe incident classification scheme does not distinguish between cybersecurity incidents and operational failures, creating ambiguity in escalation. There are no defined RTO/RPO targets linked to incident response.
  • RS.AN — Incident AnalysisForensic preservation capability is absent. In the event of a serious incident, Veriflow would have no ability to preserve evidence without engaging external support — and no retainer for such support exists.
  • RS.CO — Reporting and CommunicationStaff responsibilities for regulatory notification are not clearly assigned. There is no pre-approved communication template for customer notification following a data breach.
  • RS.MI — Incident MitigationNo pre-authorised containment actions exist that can be executed without lengthy approval chains during an active incident.

06

Recover

T1 → T2  ·  🟠 Medium-High Gap

Current State

Veriflow has a Business Continuity Plan created in 2022 and not updated since. Backup procedures exist for core transaction databases but have not been formally tested. There is no documented recovery plan specific to cybersecurity incidents. Veriflow has not assessed whether its cloud provider's shared responsibility model adequately covers its recovery objectives.

Identified Gaps

  • RC.RP — Incident Recovery Plan ExecutionNo cyber-specific recovery plan exists. The BCP does not account for scenarios where recovery is complicated by ongoing attacker presence in the environment.
  • RC.CO — Incident Recovery CommunicationNo protocol exists for communicating recovery status to staff, customers, or regulators during a prolonged incident. This gap carries regulatory risk under FCA PS21/3 operational resilience requirements.

AI Risk Overlay

Veriflow's deployment of an AI-assisted fraud detection model introduces obligations that sit at the intersection of the CSF 2.0 framework and emerging AI regulation.

Under the EU AI Act, Veriflow's AI fraud detection system is likely classifiable as a high-risk AI system under Annex III (credit and financial services). This triggers requirements for risk management systems, data governance, transparency, human oversight, and accuracy — obligations that map closely to the Govern and Identify functions of CSF 2.0.

The absence of an AI governance policy and the lack of model monitoring (identified under DE.CM) represent the most acute AI-specific gaps. These should be addressed in parallel with the general CSF remediation programme.

Arken Systems recommends Veriflow commission a separate EU AI Act conformity assessment as a companion engagement to this CSF gap analysis. See also: Arken Systems Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) for Veriflow — April 2026.

Analyst Notes

This assessment reflects the position at the time of engagement (April 2026). Veriflow's cybersecurity posture is not atypical for a fintech organisation at its growth stage — the gaps identified are serious but remediable within a 12-month programme. The most significant structural risk is the absence of board-level cybersecurity accountability and the under-resourced CISO function. Without addressing these, tactical improvements to controls will remain fragile.

The recommendation to target Tier 2 rather than Tier 3 for the Recover function reflects a pragmatic assessment of organisational capacity. Attempting to reach Tier 3 across all six functions simultaneously within 12 months would likely produce superficial compliance rather than genuine capability improvement. Veriflow should treat Tier 3 recovery capability as a 2027 objective, contingent on stabilising the Govern and Respond functions first.
