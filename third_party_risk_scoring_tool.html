Scoring Approach

The model is intentionally plain. Yes scores 1.0, Partial scores 0.5, No scores 0.0, and N/A is removed from the denominator rather than treated as failure. That means the result is easy to follow and easy to challenge. I would rather have a scoring model someone can argue with in ten minutes than a fancier one nobody fully trusts.

Domain score = sum(responses) / answered x 100
Overall score = sum(domain score x weight)
Thresholds: 80+ Low | 60+ Medium | 40+ High | under 40 Critical
Domain Weight Rationale

These weights are scenario-led rather than perfectly symmetrical. I built them around the kind of supplier that can become awkward very quickly if identity control is loose, incident response is performative, or privacy obligations are only half-understood. Some areas matter because they are foundational. Others matter because they become the problem you discover too late. Either way, I did not want weak answers in the serious places getting blurred out by a tidy average.

Domain Weight Rationale
Data Protection15%If personal data is in scope, this can turn from “process question” to “real problem” very fast.
Compliance15%Not enough on its own, but in this scenario weak independent assurance would make me slow down.
Identity & Access14%Too many ugly outcomes start with access that was broader, older, or looser than anyone realised.
Incident Response14%A vendor can sound polished right up until the day something breaks. That is why this stays high.
Supply Chain12%Fourth parties have a habit of appearing only after you ask the second uncomfortable question.
Risk Management12%I do not need elegant theory here. I want signs that somebody notices problems and owns them.
Business Continuity10%Still important, just not the first place I would probe if confidence is already wobbling elsewhere.
Asset Management8%Useful signal, but in this scenario it is more supporting evidence than decisive factor.
Approach
Why SIG Lite?
Because it gives a recognisable shape to the review without pretending this is a full procurement platform. I used it as a frame, then bent it toward the scenario.
Why weighted domains?
Because a flat average is often too forgiving. It lets uncomfortable answers hide behind tidier ones.
Why follow-up questions?
Because the interesting part usually starts after the form comes back. A weak answer matters, but the explanation matters more.
Why exclude N/A?
Because genuinely out-of-scope controls should not distort the number. But I would still inspect N/A answers carefully; they are sometimes genuine, and sometimes convenient.
Assumptions and Limitations
01Fixed question set. In real use I would want the questions reviewed periodically, because controls age and threat context moves.
02The weights are judgement calls, not immutable truth. Another organisation could weight the same domains differently and still be sensible.
03Remediation prompts only cover selected questions. That was a scope choice, not a claim that the rest are minor.
04The score does not do a sophisticated job with compensating controls. Real suppliers are sometimes better or worse than the raw answers suggest.
05This remains self-reported assurance. Useful for screening. Not enough by itself for a genuinely high-consequence supplier.
Governance Note

This is a portfolio tool, not an attempt to reduce vendor risk to one clever number. The point is to make the judgement visible: what was weighted, what was treated as especially serious, where the next challenge would go, and where I would probably stop smiling in the meeting.
