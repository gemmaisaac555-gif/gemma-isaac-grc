OSINT Human Attack Surface Mapper
Tool 03 — Arken Systems Threat Intelligence Suite

A structured way to think about the human side of attack surface. The point is not to look clever with collection tricks. It is to capture what a patient attacker could learn before touching a system at all: who works there, which roles look useful, what suppliers are visible, how the company presents itself publicly, and how much of that can be turned into a believable approach.

Methodology & design notes
What the tool is doing, where it is rough on purpose, and where I chose not to pretend the scoring is more scientific than it is.

What this tool actually is

This is a structured OSINT assessment framework for mapping the human attack surface of an organisation. It looks across people exposure, organisational detail, and public technical clues. In plain terms: what could a patient attacker learn before making contact?

It does not perform live collection. That was deliberate. Once a tool starts querying live sources, you are into different territory: scope, legality, authorisation, personal data handling, and how findings are retained. I did not want to blur that line in a portfolio piece just to make it look more sophisticated.

Scoring model

Each answer carries a simple 0–3 value. Domain scores are converted into percentages, then averaged into an overall exposure score. That score maps to four rough tiers:

Critical ≥ 75 — significant, actionable exposure across multiple areas High 55–74 — enough visible detail for targeted attacks to be plausible Medium 35–54 — noticeable exposure, but not consistently strong across the board Low < 35 — limited open-source attack surface identified

I kept the model simple on purpose. A more granular score would look more rigorous than it really is. In work like this, false precision is a genuine bad habit.

Why people-layer exposure is weighted heavily

For organisations of this size, believable contact is often a more realistic first move than technical exploitation. If an attacker can identify the finance lead, infer email addresses, name a real supplier, and sound roughly like they belong in the workflow, that is already enough to create trouble.

That is why breach data, role visibility, and publicly available staff detail are not treated as side issues here.

Where this is limited

This tool is only as good as the research entered into it. It does not assess internal weaknesses, active reconnaissance, physical security, insider threat, or how capable the organisation actually is at detecting and responding. It also does not try to model attacker intent, which matters more than some scoring systems admit.

It is mainly calibrated for organisations in roughly the 25–500 employee range. Very small firms may look artificially “low exposure” simply because they publish less. Large firms have different visibility problems and usually need a broader model than this.

Governance note

OSINT can be a legitimate part of third-party review, adversary simulation, exposure assessment, or awareness work. It still needs boundaries. Clear scope matters. Clear authority matters. Findings can also become sensitive in their own right once written down.

This is a portfolio exercise showing how that kind of assessment can be structured and explained. It is not a surveillance tool and it is not meant to masquerade as one.

Loose alignment: NCSC cyber threat intelligence guidance · PTES reconnaissance phase · MITRE ATT&CK Reconnaissance (TA0043)
