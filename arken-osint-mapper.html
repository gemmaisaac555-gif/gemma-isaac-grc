OSINT Human Attack Surface Mapper
Tool 03 — Arken Systems Threat Intelligence Suite

A structured way to assess the human side of an organisation’s attack surface. It focuses on the information a patient attacker can gather before they touch a single system: who works there, how the business is structured, what suppliers and technologies are visible, and how much of that can be turned into a believable pretext.

Domain 01 — People layer
Personnel Exposure
What can an attacker realistically learn about the organisation’s people from open sources?
LinkedIn & Org Chart Exposure
People intelligence · Role mapping · Targeting
▼
Why this mattersLinkedIn is often the attacker’s starting point. Public profiles reveal names, roles, tenure, career history, and sometimes reporting lines. That is enough to identify who handles finance, who sits in IT, who is senior enough to impersonate, and who is likely to trust whom. By the time the first phishing email arrives, the attacker may already understand the org chart surprisingly well.
Email Format & Address Inference
Credential intelligence · Phishing enablement
▼
Why this mattersOnce an attacker knows how the organisation formats email addresses, they do not need a directory. A single visible address can reveal the pattern for everyone else. Combined with names from LinkedIn or a company website, that is enough to build a targeted phishing list very quickly. The format is often exposed accidentally through press releases, conference bios, research papers, or old breach data.
Social Media & Personal Exposure
Pretext intelligence · Personal targeting
▼
Why this mattersPersonal social media often gives away far more than people realise: interests, routines, travel, frustrations, colleagues, and family context. None of that has to be especially dramatic to be useful. Social engineering usually works because the pretext feels familiar, not because it is technically sophisticated.
Domain 02 — Organisational intelligence
Organisational Exposure
What can an attacker piece together about the organisation itself from public information?
Job Postings & Tech Stack Exposure
Technology intelligence · Vulnerability targeting
▼
Why this mattersJob adverts can be one of the richest intelligence sources an organisation publishes without meaning to. A role asking for AWS, Okta, Palo Alto, CrowdStrike, Salesforce, or a particular SIEM stack tells an attacker where to focus. Even when the advert seems routine, it can still narrow the attacker’s assumptions dramatically.
Companies House & Public Filings
Director intelligence · Corporate structure
▼
Why this mattersUK public filings expose real structure: directors, officers, filing history, and older address information. Much of this is unavoidable, but that does not make it harmless. When public records are combined with LinkedIn or social media, they can create a much clearer targeting picture than most organisations expect.
Supplier & Partner Relationships
Supply chain attack surface · Trust relationship exploitation
▼
Why this mattersNamed suppliers, implementation partners, and known platforms all widen the options for impersonation. If an attacker knows who provides invoicing, hosting, payroll, IT support, or onboarding software, they can borrow that trust. That is often enough to make a suspicious request seem ordinary.
Domain 03 — Digital footprint
Digital Footprint & Technical Exposure

Domain & Certificate Intelligence
Infrastructure mapping · Subdomain exposure
▼
Why this mattersCertificate transparency logs and related domain intelligence often reveal more than organisations expect: staging, development, admin, or other non-production environments that were never meant to be prominent. These do not automatically equal compromise, but they do reduce the attacker’s search space and help them understand how the estate is put together.
Code Repository & GitHub Exposure
Credential leakage · Architecture intelligence
▼
Why this mattersPublic repositories are not just a developer issue. They can expose API keys, configuration files, internal hostnames, architecture notes, and other clues that move an attacker from guesswork into specifics. Even where there is no direct secret leakage, repository content can still reveal how the environment works.
Conference, Publication & Media Exposure

Why this mattersConference talks, interviews, case studies, podcasts, and published articles can all become reconnaissance material. Even when the content is perfectly legitimate, it may still reveal strategy, security priorities, internal challenges, supplier choices, or architecture patterns. That can be enough to make later targeting much more convincing.

Methodology & Design Decisions
What this tool is doing, what it is not doing, and how the scoring should be read.

What this tool is

This is a structured OSINT assessment framework for mapping the human attack surface of an organisation. It looks across three areas: people-layer exposure, organisational intelligence, and digital footprint. In practice, that means asking what a patient attacker could learn about staff, structure, suppliers, technologies, and public-facing assets before making contact or attempting compromise.

It does not perform live collection. Instead, it is designed for someone who has already done the research and now needs a clear way to record findings, score them, and explain why they matter in risk terms rather than just listing observations.

Scoring model

Each question is scored from 0 to 3 based on the selected exposure level. Scores are averaged within each domain and then combined into an overall percentage score from 0 to 100, which is mapped to four exposure tiers:

Critical ≥ 75 — Significant, actionable attack surface across multiple domains High 55–74 — Material exposure in at least one domain, targeted attacks are feasible Medium 35–54 — Noticeable exposure, opportunistic or partly targeted attacks are plausible Low < 35 — Limited open-source attack surface identified

The model deliberately gives real weight to people and organisational intelligence, because for many organisations of this size the first successful move is more likely to be social engineering than direct technical exploitation.

Design decisions

Why use a structured framework instead of live OSINT queries? Because live collection changes the nature of the tool. It raises questions about scope, authorisation, legality, and handling of personal data. A structured framework is more honest about what it is: an assessment layer, not a surveillance utility.

Why these three domains? Because they reflect the main reconnaissance layers an attacker often works through: people, organisation, and digital footprint. Technical attack-surface scanning is related, but it is a different activity with different tooling and different assumptions.

Why include breach credential data in the people layer? Because the risk is still fundamentally person-centred. Breach data helps an attacker target individuals, test reused credentials, or build stronger phishing campaigns. It is discovered through OSINT, but the impact still lands on named people and their access.

Assumptions and limitations

This tool focuses on passive, open-source exposure. It does not assess active reconnaissance, internal technical weaknesses, physical security, insider threat, or the real-world effectiveness of the organisation’s security team. It is only as good as the research entered into it.

It is mainly calibrated for organisations of roughly 25 to 500 employees, especially in regulated or semi-regulated sectors. Larger organisations have different visibility problems. Very small organisations may appear “low exposure” simply because they have less public footprint overall, not because they have made especially strong choices.

Governance note

OSINT can be a legitimate part of third-party risk review, adversary simulation, security awareness work, or internal exposure assessment. It should still be handled carefully: clear scope, clear authorisation, and an understanding that the findings themselves may be sensitive. This tool is a portfolio exercise showing how that kind of assessment can be structured and explained — not a tool for covert collection or surveillance.
