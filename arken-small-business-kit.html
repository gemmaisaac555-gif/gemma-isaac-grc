About This Tool
What it is, how it works, and why it is built this way.

What this tool is

This is a small-business security checklist tool built around a trades-business scenario. It asks a short set of questions, then turns the answers into a prioritised action list, a printable checklist, and a draft staff email.

The format is deliberate. Small firms are not short of guidance; they are short of time, patience, and someone to turn security advice into something usable on an ordinary workday.

Why plain English throughout

An earlier version of the phone policy sounded the way these things often do: tidy, formal, and not very readable. One of the engineers glanced at it and basically treated it like something to sign and forget. Fair enough. That was useful feedback.

If the people using the phones are not going to read the document, the wording is part of the problem. In a small business, clarity matters more than polish. A shorter rule that people actually follow is worth more than a nicer one they ignore.

How recommendations are prioritised

Recommendations are generated from five scored inputs: MFA status, screen lock status, password manager status, email system, and data handling practices. The first priority tier focuses on the gaps that most often make small incidents worse than they needed to be: no MFA, weak or missing device locks, and poor password habits.

The order also reflects a simple practical point. The best early controls are usually the ones that are cheap, quick, and hard to argue against. Turning on MFA and sorting out phone locks is not the whole answer, but it changes the baseline fast.

What the cost and effort assumptions are doing here

The cost and time assumptions in this tool are grounded in a realistic small-business rollout rather than an enterprise programme. The point is not that every firm will spend the same amount. They will not. The point is that the first useful controls are usually cheap, and the bigger barrier is getting round to doing them.

In this example, certification is the only obvious cash cost. Most of the rest is admin time: checking phones, turning on MFA, sorting out passwords, writing down what the rules are, and explaining the changes to staff. None of that is glamorous. It is also where most small businesses actually improve.

Key decisions in the NorthWest Heating example

Company phones rather than full BYOD for engineers: this was mostly a practical decision. Phones get left in vans, dropped on jobs, borrowed in a rush, and occasionally go missing. It was simpler to treat the work phone as work property and set expectations from there.

Setup before the announcement: most of the fiddly account and phone changes were done first. That mattered. It is easier to get buy-in when people are not being handed a problem disguised as a policy.

Fingerprint plus PIN, not a complicated password every time: this was a usability call as much as a security one. Engineers unlock phones constantly. If the setup is annoying, people work around it. That does happen.

Bitwarden rather than paying for something heavier: the free tier covered what they needed. That is not a universal answer. It was enough here.

Assumptions and limitations

This is calibrated for small UK trades and field-service businesses, roughly in the 2–25 employee range. Recommendations are based on NCSC Cyber Essentials and ICO guidance. Businesses in more heavily regulated sectors will have additional requirements this tool does not try to cover.

NorthWest Heating is a realistic composite scenario rather than a real company. That gives the worked example enough detail to be useful without pretending every business starts from exactly the same place.

Why the outputs look like this

The design of this tool is part of the point. Most small businesses do not need a heavyweight security platform first. They need a short path from “we should sort this out” to “right, here is what we are doing this week.”

That is why the outputs are basic on purpose: priorities, checklist, staff message, plain language. Better guidance is not always more detailed guidance. Sometimes it is just guidance that gets used.
