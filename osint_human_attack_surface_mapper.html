Tool 03 — Arken Systems Threat Intelligence Suite

A structured framework for assessing the open-source exposure of a target organisation. It looks at people, organisational detail, and digital footprint — in other words, the sort of material an attacker may gather before they ever touch the environment. This is deliberately slower and more reflective than live tooling. The point is judgement, not scraping.

Domain 01 — People layer
Personnel Exposure
What can be learned about the organisation’s people from open sources?
LinkedIn & Org Chart Exposure
People intelligence · role mapping · targeting
▼
Why this matters LinkedIn is one of the easiest ways to turn a company into a list of names, roles, and reporting lines. That sounds harmless until you remember how much fraud and phishing begins with exactly that sort of map. If I can work out who approves invoices, who runs IT, who just joined, and who reports to whom, I do not need perfect information. I need enough.
Email Format & Address Inference
Credential intelligence · phishing enablement
▼
Why this matters Once names are visible, email format becomes the missing piece. Sometimes that format is handed away in a press release footer or a conference bio. Sometimes it only takes one visible address. From there, the jump to a plausible staff list is trivial.
Social Media & Personal Exposure
Pretext intelligence · personal targeting
▼
Why this matters Oversharing is not really the issue on its own. The problem is aggregation. A holiday post, a charity event photo, a comment about working late, a conference selfie, a mention of a child’s school pickup — each detail may look harmless in isolation. Together they can make a fraudulent message feel uncomfortably real.
Domain 02 — Organisational intelligence
Organisational Exposure
What does an external party learn about the organisation itself from open sources?
Job Postings & Tech Stack Exposure
Technology intelligence · vulnerability targeting
▼
Why this matters Hiring ads often tell a stranger more than the company realises. A stack named casually for recruitment reasons can become reconnaissance material. This does not mean every job advert should be vague nonsense, but there is usually a middle ground between “competitive role in engineering” and “here is the exact environment we would rather you did not map.”
Companies House & Public Filings
Director intelligence · corporate structure
▼
Why this matters Companies House is public record, so this is not a criticism of publishing it. The question is what becomes easy once those records are combined with everything else. Director names, filing history, old addresses in legacy documents, insolvency noise, dormant entities, linked appointments — none of this is dramatic on its own. It still helps build a picture.
Supplier & Partner Relationships
Supply chain attack surface · trust relationship exploitation
▼
Why this matters This is one of the quieter risks. Public partner pages, client case studies, procurement notices, implementation awards — they all tell an outsider who the organisation is used to hearing from. That matters because impersonation works best when it borrows trust that already exists.
Domain 03 — Digital footprint
Digital Footprint & Technical Exposure

Domain & Certificate Intelligence
Infrastructure mapping · subdomain exposure
▼
Why this matters Certificate transparency logs have a habit of showing more than teams expect: staging names, admin panels, forgotten subdomains, bits of internal naming logic that were never intended to be public. Not every exposed hostname is dangerous. Some are just messy. Attackers still like messy.
Code Repository & GitHub Exposure
Credential leakage · architecture intelligence
▼
Why this matters Public repositories can leak credentials, but that is not the only risk. Old commits, internal hostnames, half-removed configuration, comments written for colleagues rather than strangers — all of that can be useful. Even when there is no direct secret exposure, a repo can still explain how the place hangs together.
Conference, Publication & Media Exposure
Authority targeting · pretext material
▼
Why this matters People often worry about the one dramatic overshare. More often it is cumulative. A conference talk mentions tooling, a podcast mentions growth pressure, a case study names a supplier, a blog post shows a screenshot, and suddenly an outsider has a decent working model of the environment. No single piece did the damage. The pile did.

Methodology & Design Decisions
How this tool works, what it assesses, and what it does not.

What this tool is

A structured OSINT assessment framework for mapping the human attack surface of a target organisation. It covers three domains: people-layer exposure, organisational intelligence, and digital footprint.

The tool does not perform live data collection. It is used after manual research has already been done, so the assessor can record findings and turn them into something reviewable. That sounds less glamorous than live tooling. It is also more realistic for a governance portfolio piece.

Scoring model

Each domain question is scored 0–3 based on the selected exposure level. Domain scores are averaged and converted into a percentage of maximum possible exposure. The final score is expressed on a 0–100 scale with four tiers:

Critical ≥ 75 — Significant, actionable attack surface across multiple domains High 55–74 — Material exposure in at least one domain, targeted attacks feasible Medium 35–54 — Limited exposure, opportunistic attacks possible Low < 35 — Minimal open-source attack surface identified

Strictly speaking, this is not mathematically sophisticated. It is a judgement scaffold. That is enough for the point being made here.

The output leans slightly toward people and organisational intelligence rather than pure technical footprint. For firms of this size, many realistic attack paths still begin with social engineering, impersonation, or workflow manipulation rather than with direct exploitation.

Design decisions

Why a structured framework rather than live queries? Because the minute this becomes a live collection tool, it becomes a different kind of project with different ethical and legal implications.

Why these three domains? They are the three reconnaissance layers I wanted to force into one view: who the people are, what the organisation accidentally says about itself, and what the digital footprint gives away without being asked.

Why is breach credential data included in the people layer? Because in practice it changes the targeting of named individuals, even if the source is an external dataset rather than the organisation’s own publication.

Assumptions and limitations

This tool assesses passive, open-source exposure only. It does not assess active reconnaissance, technical vulnerability scanning, physical security, or insider threat.

Scores depend heavily on the quality of the underlying research. Bad input gives tidy-looking bad output. That is worth saying plainly.

The tool is broadly calibrated for organisations of roughly 25–500 employees in regulated or semi-regulated sectors. Smaller firms can still have ugly exposure, but the pattern often looks different.

Governance note

OSINT assessment can be useful in third-party risk review, red team preparation, or awareness work. It should be carried out within a defined scope, with proper authorisation, and with findings handled as sensitive intelligence. This is a portfolio exercise demonstrating assessment method. It is not a surveillance tool, and it is not pretending to be one.
