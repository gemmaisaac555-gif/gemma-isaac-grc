Regulator Lens — How I Think

If I Were the ICO:
Reviewing Veriflow's AI Credit Scoring Deployment

What would the Information Commissioner's Office actually look for — and what would concern them most? This is not a compliance checklist. It is an attempt to reason from inside an enforcement perspective.

Gemma Isaac · April 2026 · Portfolio — How I Think

There is a difference between knowing what the ICO's guidance says and understanding how the ICO thinks. The guidance describes obligations. Enforcement priorities describe what actually gets investigated, what generates formal reprimands, and what keeps a Deputy Commissioner awake at night. If you only understand the first, you will build compliance programmes that satisfy documentation requirements while missing the things regulators genuinely care about.

This piece is an exercise in inhabiting the second. I am going to walk through Veriflow Payments' deployment of the ClearScore AI credit scoring system as if I were an ICO caseworker conducting a preliminary assessment. I will draw on the ICO's published enforcement actions, their AI and automated decision-making guidance, their recent work on algorithmic transparency, and the accountability framework under UK GDPR. I will also draw on what I know about Veriflow's actual governance position from the risk register, FRIA, and vendor due diligence work done as part of this portfolio.

The honest framing: this is a thinking exercise, not a real investigation. But the discipline of taking a regulator's perspective rather than a compliance officer's perspective is genuinely useful, and not just for interview purposes.

What the ICO would look for first

ICO investigations into automated decision-making typically start with two questions before they get anywhere near the technical detail of the model. The first is: does the organisation know what it is doing? Not in a philosophical sense — in a practical, documented sense. Can they explain what data the model uses, how it produces outputs, and what those outputs mean for the people affected by them? The second is: has anyone with authority taken responsibility for this? Governance failures are as concerning to the ICO as technical failures, and in many enforcement actions they are the primary finding.

Veriflow would pass the first question reasonably well. The model card documents intended use, known limitations, and human oversight structure. The FRIA exists and is not superficial. There is documented awareness of the regulatory obligations. Where Veriflow would struggle is on the second question — the CISO function is under-resourced and dual-hatted with technology delivery. An ICO caseworker would note that there is no dedicated accountability for AI system governance sitting at a level that can actually enforce decisions. The model card names the Chief Risk Officer as document owner. Whether the CRO has the authority and resource to act on what the document says is a different question.

ICO Enforcement Pattern — What this maps to

The ICO's 2021 reprimand of Clearview AI and its 2023 investigation into facial recognition at King's Cross both turned significantly on the question of whether senior leadership understood and owned the risk being run. Documentation without ownership is not accountability under UK GDPR Article 5(2). The ICO has been explicit about this in enforcement correspondence.

The five things that would concern me most

01
The retrospective FRIA The FRIA was completed after deployment, not before. The EU AI Act requires pre-deployment assessment for high-risk systems. The ICO, while enforcing UK GDPR rather than the EU AI Act directly, has adopted the position that a DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) for automated decision-making systems should be completed before the system processes personal data. A retrospective FRIA signals that the governance decision was made first and the assessment was conducted to satisfy documentation requirements afterwards. That is the opposite of what Article 35 of UK GDPR requires, and it is the pattern the ICO looks for when assessing whether an organisation is genuinely privacy-by-design or merely privacy-by-documentation.
02
The explanation gap Veriflow's affected individuals receive feature importance outputs — the factors that contributed to their credit decision. The ICO's guidance on AI and data protection is clear that explanations under Article 22(3) must be "meaningful" — not just technically present. Feature importance scores derived from a black-box model are not the same as an explanation of the decision. The ICO has signalled, in its guidance on explaining AI decisions, that it will look at whether explanations are genuinely useful to the person receiving them. Veriflow has not validated whether its explanation outputs meet this standard. That is a gap an ICO assessor would flag immediately.
03
No independent bias monitoring Veriflow's monitoring of the model for discriminatory outcomes is entirely dependent on ClearScore AI's quarterly reporting. The ICO's guidance on AI fairness is explicit that controllers cannot outsource accountability for discriminatory outcomes to their processors. The fact that Veriflow does not hold demographic data makes this harder, not easier — it means Veriflow cannot demonstrate compliance with the fairness requirements even if asked to. The ICO would view this not as a mitigating factor but as a governance failure: if you cannot monitor for discriminatory outcomes, you should not be deploying a system that could produce them.
04
The appeals gap UK GDPR Article 22 requires that individuals subject to solely automated decisions have the right to obtain human intervention, express their point of view, and contest the decision. As of the model card's last update, Veriflow had not documented an appeals process. The model card notes this as an open remediation item. An ICO caseworker reviewing this would note that a legally required right has not been operationalised — not a documentation failure but a substantive failure of data subject rights. This is the kind of gap that generates enforcement action rather than informal guidance.
05
Sub-processor opacity Veriflow does not have full visibility into ClearScore AI's sub-processors — the infrastructure providers, data enrichment services, and other third parties whose processing forms part of the overall data flow. Under UK GDPR Article 28, a controller must ensure that processors only engage sub-processors with prior consent, and that sub-processors are subject to equivalent data protection obligations. Veriflow cannot demonstrate this chain of accountability because it does not know the full chain. The ICO has pursued sub-processor failures in enforcement — it is not an abstract concern.

The ICO does not investigate because organisations have imperfect documentation. It investigates because someone was harmed, or because a pattern suggests harm is likely. The question to ask is not "can we show a document?" but "can we show we actually protected people?"

What would mitigate an investigation

ICO investigations are resource-intensive and the ICO is selective. Organisations that demonstrate genuine engagement with their obligations — even where there are gaps — fare better than organisations that have comprehensive documentation but no substance behind it. The distinction between the two is usually visible within minutes of reviewing an organisation's records.

For Veriflow, the mitigating factors are real. The FRIA exists, is substantive, and acknowledges limitations. The model card is honest about compliance gaps and treats them as live remediation items rather than resolved. The vendor due diligence identified specific contractual requirements and those requirements were actually included in the contract. The risk register is current. These are signals that an organisation is trying to do this properly, even where it falls short.

The ICO's approach to enforcement — set out in its Regulatory Action Policy — places weight on proportionality and on whether an organisation has demonstrated a commitment to compliance. An organisation that identified its Article 22 appeals gap and has a documented plan to close it is in a materially better position than one that doesn't know the gap exists.

The honest conclusion from this exercise: Veriflow is not a compliance disaster. It is a fintech organisation at a growth stage that has done more governance work than most, has documented its limitations honestly, and has meaningful gaps in the places that matter most — monitoring, appeals, and senior accountability. An ICO investigation prompted by a discrimination complaint would find an organisation that knew its risks and didn't fully close them. That is a better story than many, and a worse story than it needed to be.
