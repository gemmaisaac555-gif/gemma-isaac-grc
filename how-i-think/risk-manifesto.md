# Risk Manifesto
*Gemma Isaac — GRC Portfolio*

---

I came to risk management sideways. My training was in history and languages — disciplines built around understanding how systems fail, how institutions misread situations, how the same event looks entirely different depending on who is telling the story and what they need it to mean. I spent years in archives, working with records that organisations created to document decisions, and sometimes to insulate themselves from accountability for those decisions. Then a stroke at twenty-nine rerouted everything.

That background shapes how I think about risk. Not as a technical domain that happens to involve people, but as a fundamentally human problem that happens to have technical components.

---

**Controls fail at the human layer first.**

The gap in most security incidents is not a missing firewall rule or an unpatched system. It is a person under pressure making a decision that felt reasonable in the moment, inside a system that made the unsafe action the easier path. Policies exist. Controls exist. The question is whether they hold when someone is tired, rushed, confused, or being manipulated by someone who has done their homework. I design for that version of reality, not the version where everyone reads the policy and follows it correctly.

**Frameworks are tools for thinking, not substitutes for it.**

I use NIST, ISO 27001, and other frameworks because they encode accumulated experience and provide a shared language for governance conversations. I do not use them as checklists. A framework applied without understanding the organisation's specific context, threat landscape, and risk appetite produces the appearance of assurance rather than assurance itself. The most dangerous artefact in any GRC programme is a completed compliance checklist that nobody has actually thought about.

**A risk register nobody reads is a liability, not an asset.**

I have seen risk registers that are genuinely useful governance documents. I have seen risk registers that exist to demonstrate that risk management is happening. The difference is not length or format — it is whether the people accountable for the risks named in the register treat it as a live record of decisions under uncertainty, or as a document produced for auditors and filed. I write risk registers for the first audience.

**Accepting risk honestly is better than pretending to eliminate it.**

Residual risk is real. Budgets are finite. Priorities compete. The right response to a risk you cannot fully mitigate is not to score it down until it looks acceptable — it is to document what you know, name who carries the exposure if it materialises, and be clear about what would change your assessment. Organisations that practise honest risk acceptance make better decisions over time than those that manufacture comfort through optimistic scoring.

**Context is the first thing I look for.**

A control proportionate for a Series B fintech is not the same control proportionate for a defence supply chain contractor. Regulatory language that makes sense in financial services lands differently in healthcare. Before I assess risk or recommend a control, I want to understand the business, the people, the incentive structures, and what is genuinely at stake if something goes wrong. That understanding changes everything that follows.

---

*I approach uncertainty by being precise about what I do and do not know, honest about the assumptions I am making, and clear about what evidence would change my view. I am more suspicious of confident risk assessments than uncertain ones.*
