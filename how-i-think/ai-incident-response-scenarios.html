AI Incident Response:
Three Scenarios from the Field

Real incidents don't arrive with complete information. These scenarios reconstruct three AI system failures at Veriflow Payments Ltd — each with partial data, time pressure, and a decision that had to be made anyway.

These are not post-hoc analyses. They are written as live incident responses — in the moment, with what was known at the time. The uncertainty is intentional. Decisions made with acknowledged gaps are more realistic than frameworks applied after the fact.

Prepared by Gemma Isaac, GRC Analyst, Arken Systems · April 2026
Credit Bias Escalation:
Disproportionate Decline Rates in Ethnic Minority Applicants
14 March 2026 · 09:47
ClearScore AI Credit Scoring
Head of Customer Operations
VF-AI-INC-001
● Critical
How this reached me
Mon 09:12
Customer Operations receives a complaint from a rejected merchant applicant alleging racial discrimination in the credit decision. Complaint references two friends from similar financial backgrounds who were accepted. Complaint
09:31
Customer Operations manager pulls the last 30 days of decline decisions and notices, informally, that a higher proportion of declined applicants have names suggesting South Asian and Black African origin. She flags to her director. Pattern flag
09:47
I receive an email: "Gemma — can you look at this? Not sure if it's a data thing or a model thing or nothing. Don't want to escalate if it's nothing." No data attached. No statistical analysis. Just the observation. Referred to GRC
10:15
I pull the available internal data. Veriflow does not collect applicant ethnicity data directly — this information is not in our systems. The pattern observation was based on names, which is not a reliable proxy and is itself a concerning analytical approach. Data gap
11:00
I contact ClearScore AI to request disaggregated performance data by demographic group. They confirm this is in their quarterly reporting cycle. Next report due in six weeks. No ad-hoc reporting capability offered. Vendor gap

What I know

  • One formal discrimination complaint has been received and is live
  • A pattern observation exists — but it was made informally and without statistical rigour
  • Veriflow does not hold ethnicity data so cannot independently verify a demographic disparity
  • ClearScore AI's vendor due diligence identified that bias monitoring is vendor-controlled with no deployer-side visibility
  • The AI risk register flagged disproportionate impact as a High risk with a mitigation action that has not yet been completed
  • Under the Equality Act 2010, indirect discrimination does not require intent

What I don't know

  • Whether there is a statistically significant disparity — the informal pattern observation is not evidence
  • Whether the disparity, if real, is driven by the model or by input data correlated with protected characteristics
  • How many applicants may have been affected — we have no way to calculate this without demographic data
  • Whether ClearScore AI's training data contains proxies for ethnicity (postcode, spending patterns) that create indirect discrimination
  • Whether the individual complaint has merit — that requires a separate review of the specific decision
  • What ClearScore AI's actual fairness metrics show — we are six weeks from their next report

Regulatory implications

Equality Act 2010
Indirect discrimination — if the model produces disparate outcomes for a protected group without justification, this is unlawful regardless of intent. A single complaint is sufficient to trigger investigation obligations.
FCA Consumer Duty
The duty requires firms to deliver good outcomes for all customers, including those sharing protected characteristics. An unexplained disparity in credit decisions is a Consumer Duty concern even before it constitutes discrimination.
EU AI Act Art. 26
As deployer, Veriflow is responsible for monitoring AI system outputs for compliance and for taking action where risks to fundamental rights are identified. We cannot outsource this obligation to ClearScore AI.
UK GDPR Art. 22
The complainant has a right to meaningful explanation of the automated decision affecting them. This request may follow. We need to be able to provide one.
My decision and the reasoning behind it
I cannot confirm whether a discriminatory pattern exists. The informal observation is not evidence. I am making decisions based on the possibility that it is real, because the consequences of acting too slowly — if it is real — are materially worse than acting on something that turns out to be noise.

The first and most important decision is to treat this as a live incident from this moment, not as a monitoring flag. The distinction matters: a live incident triggers our incident response process, creates a documented timeline, and assigns ownership. A monitoring flag can sit on a tracker for six weeks. Given the regulatory exposure — Equality Act, Consumer Duty, EU AI Act — I am not prepared to let this sit.

The second decision is to not suspend the model immediately. This is the harder call and I want to be explicit about my reasoning. Suspension would be the appropriate response if I had evidence of ongoing harm. I do not yet have that evidence — I have one complaint and an informal observation. Suspension would also cause significant operational disruption and would need to be justified to senior leadership. I am not confident I can justify it on current information. I may be wrong about this. If the disparity is confirmed, I will need to defend this decision.

The third decision is to invoke contractual audit rights immediately. Our vendor due diligence established these rights. I am exercising them now — not in six weeks when the quarterly report arrives. I am requesting disaggregated performance data by postcode cluster (as a proxy for demographic risk) within five business days. If ClearScore AI does not provide this, that itself becomes an escalation point.

Actions, in priority order

01
Log this as a formal AI incident (VF-AI-INC-001) and notify the CISO and DPO within the hour
Creates the documented timeline. If this escalates, we need to show we took it seriously immediately.
02
Request emergency disaggregated performance data from ClearScore AI under our contractual audit rights — five business day deadline
We cannot assess severity without data. The vendor contractual obligation exists; use it.
03
Review the specific declined application that generated the complaint and prepare a decision explanation for the complainant
UK GDPR Art. 22 right to explanation. Also: if this application was incorrectly declined, we should know that now.
04
Establish an independent monitoring layer for decline rates by postcode cluster — do not wait for vendor data
This is a gap we identified in vendor due diligence and never closed. Close it now.
05
Schedule a 48-hour review point — if ClearScore AI data confirms disparity, I will recommend model suspension at that point
I am not suspending now on insufficient evidence. I am committing to a decision point when evidence arrives.

What I'm sitting with

The decision not to suspend immediately is the one I'm least comfortable with. My reasoning is sound — I don't have evidence of ongoing harm, and suspension on the basis of an informal observation would be hard to defend. But I'm aware that "hard to defend" is a business consideration, and "someone may be experiencing discrimination right now" is a human one. I've logged that tension explicitly because I think it belongs in the incident record.

I'm also aware that the gap in our vendor contract — no ad-hoc demographic reporting capability — was identified in our due diligence and flagged as a risk. This incident is that risk materialising. The lesson is that a contractual right to annual audit data is not the same as operational visibility. We needed ongoing monitoring and we didn't build it. That's on us, not the vendor.

Model Drift Causing Financial Loss:
Approval Rate Collapse in SME Segment
7 April 2026 · 14:22
ClearScore AI Credit Scoring
Head of Commercial
VF-AI-INC-002
● Critical
How this reached me
Mon 08:30
Weekly commercial dashboard shows SME merchant approval rate has dropped from 67% to 41% over the past three weeks. Finance flags a £340k projected revenue shortfall if the rate holds. Revenue impact
09:45
Head of Commercial raises with CTO: "The model is rejecting applications it was approving six weeks ago. Same applicant profiles, different outcomes. Something has changed." Behaviour change
11:00
CTO contacts ClearScore AI. They confirm a model update was deployed three weeks ago — the same week the approval rate started falling. The update was not communicated to Veriflow in advance. Contract breach
14:22
Incident referred to GRC. Question from CTO: "Can we roll back? Should we suspend? What do we do?" No rollback has been offered by ClearScore AI yet. Current decisions are still being made by the updated model. GRC engaged

What I know

  • The model was updated three weeks ago without prior notification to Veriflow — this is a breach of the contractual notification requirement from our vendor due diligence
  • The approval rate change is statistically significant — 26 percentage points is not noise
  • The change correlates precisely with the model update timing
  • Veriflow has suffered a quantifiable financial impact — £340k projected shortfall
  • We have no information about what changed in the update or why
  • Decisions made since the update may be systematically different from what the previous model would have produced

What I don't know

  • Whether the new model is more accurate — the approval rate drop may reflect genuinely higher-risk applicants, or it may reflect miscalibration
  • Whether the applicants declined in the past three weeks were correctly declined or incorrectly declined
  • Whether a rollback is technically possible, and if so, how long it would take
  • What changed in the model update — ClearScore AI has not yet provided technical details
  • Whether other lenders using ClearScore AI have seen similar patterns
  • Whether the update introduced bias as a side effect of optimising for a different performance metric

Regulatory implications

EU AI Act Art. 26
Deployer obligation to monitor post-market performance. The fact that we detected this through revenue impact rather than monitoring is itself a governance failure. We were not actively watching the model.
FCA Consumer Duty
If the updated model is incorrectly declining creditworthy applicants, those applicants are being harmed. The duty requires good outcomes. We are potentially producing bad ones at scale right now.
Contract — ClearScore AI
The vendor due diligence established a contractual requirement for pre-deployment notification of model updates. That requirement was not met. This is a breach. It has direct financial consequences for Veriflow.
EU AI Act Art. 72
Post-market monitoring obligations. A material change in model outputs constitutes a significant event that may require regulatory notification depending on the severity assessment.
My decision and the reasoning behind it
I don't know whether the new model is right or wrong. The approval rate drop might reflect genuine risk recalibration — in which case stopping it would be the wrong call. Or it might reflect a miscalibrated update — in which case continuing it is causing harm. I am making a decision without knowing which is true.

This situation is different from the bias incident. There, I had insufficient evidence to act. Here, I have direct evidence of a material change in model behaviour — a 26 percentage point shift in outcomes — caused by an undisclosed vendor action that breached our contract. That changes the calculus.

My recommendation is to suspend the updated model and revert to manual decision-making for new applications within 24 hours, while we obtain the information needed to assess whether the update was appropriate. This is a significant operational recommendation. I am making it because we are currently running a system whose behaviour has changed materially in ways we cannot explain, and we are making credit decisions that may be systematically wrong — either too restrictive or in ways that create regulatory exposure. The cost of continuing while we investigate is the risk of ongoing harm at scale. The cost of suspending is operational disruption and some lost revenue.

On the contract breach: ClearScore AI's failure to provide pre-deployment notification is not just a process failure — it directly caused us to be in this position. I am recommending we issue a formal breach notice and that commercial leadership considers whether the contract value should be adjusted to account for the financial impact. This needs Legal involved.

Actions, in priority order

01
Recommend model suspension to CISO and CTO — implement manual review process for new applications within 24 hours
We cannot assess whether ongoing decisions are correct. Suspension is proportionate to the uncertainty.
02
Issue formal breach notice to ClearScore AI — require full technical documentation of the update within 48 hours
We need to understand what changed. The contractual breach creates leverage. Use it.
03
Commission retrospective review of decisions made since the update — sample 50 declined applications for manual assessment
If incorrectly declined applicants can be identified and contacted, we may be able to remediate. This also builds the evidence base for the regulatory question.
04
Notify DPO and assess whether FCA notification is required under operational resilience reporting
A material unexplained change in credit decision outputs may constitute a notifiable event. Better to assess and decide not to notify than to miss a notification obligation.
05
Begin evaluation of alternative credit scoring vendors — do not wait for this incident to resolve before assessing dependency risk
This is the second significant failure from ClearScore AI. Vendor concentration risk is now a board-level concern.

What I'm sitting with

The recommendation to suspend is the right call analytically, but I'm aware it will face pushback. "We're losing £340k in revenue and your solution is to take the model offline" is a hard message. My answer is that we don't know whether the model is producing the right outcomes, and continuing to make high-stakes credit decisions on a system whose behaviour we cannot explain is not acceptable — regardless of the short-term revenue cost.

The deeper concern here is systemic. We detected this incident because of revenue impact, not because we were monitoring model behaviour. That means the monitoring gap we identified in our risk register was real and consequential. If the model had drifted in a direction that was harder to see in the numbers — a subtle increase in bias, for example — we might not have caught it at all. The incident is resolved by suspension. The underlying problem is resolved by building real-time monitoring into our deployer infrastructure. That should be a post-incident commitment, not a future consideration.

Third-Party Model Failure:
ClearScore AI Service Disruption — Suspected Data Breach
22 April 2026 · 07:15
ClearScore AI Credit Scoring
IT Operations (automated alert)
VF-AI-INC-003
● High
How this reached me
Tue 06:43
Automated monitoring detects that the ClearScore AI API endpoint is returning 503 errors. Credit scoring requests for merchant applications are failing. New applications cannot be processed. Service down
07:00
IT Operations attempts to contact ClearScore AI via their support channel. Automated response only. No human contact available. Status page shows "Investigating" with no detail. Vendor unresponsive
07:15
I am paged. Simultaneously, a security researcher posts on LinkedIn that a major UK credit scoring API provider has suffered a data exposure — partial training data and customer application records alleged to be circulating on a dark web forum. No vendor named yet. Possible breach
07:42
The LinkedIn post is shared in a fintech Slack community. Someone in the thread says "pretty sure it's ClearScore AI — same infrastructure fingerprint." This is unverified. Could be speculation. Could be accurate. Unverified intel
08:00
ClearScore AI sends an email: "We are experiencing a service disruption and are investigating. We will provide an update within 4 hours." No mention of a data breach. No mention of the social media claims. Vendor comms

What I know

  • ClearScore AI's service is currently down — Veriflow cannot process new credit applications
  • ClearScore AI has acknowledged a "service disruption" but has not mentioned a data breach
  • There are public claims of a data breach affecting a UK credit scoring API provider, with ClearScore AI named speculatively but not confirmed
  • Veriflow transmits applicant financial data to ClearScore AI for scoring — if they have been breached, that data may be affected
  • We have a contractual notification requirement — ClearScore AI must notify us of security incidents
  • Our vendor due diligence identified limited sub-processor visibility as a risk — we still don't know ClearScore AI's infrastructure dependencies

What I don't know

  • Whether a data breach has actually occurred — the social media claims are unverified and may be inaccurate or speculative
  • Whether Veriflow applicant data is among any data exposed, if a breach did occur
  • The scope and nature of any breach — application data, model training data, or infrastructure access
  • When service will be restored — the 4-hour update window may or may not hold
  • Whether ClearScore AI is managing this as a security incident or purely as a service outage
  • Whether other Veriflow data transmissions to ClearScore AI are affected beyond credit scoring

Regulatory implications

UK GDPR Art. 33
If personal data of Veriflow applicants has been exposed, Veriflow as controller has a 72-hour notification obligation to the ICO — running from when Veriflow becomes aware, not when the breach is confirmed. The clock may already be running.
FCA PS21/3
Operational resilience obligations require Veriflow to maintain the ability to process applications within defined tolerance levels. The current service disruption may breach those tolerances depending on duration.
EU AI Act Art. 26
Deployer obligations include ensuring appropriate human oversight when AI systems cannot operate. We need a manual fallback process activated now — not after the vendor resolves the outage.
Contract — ClearScore AI
If a breach has occurred and ClearScore AI has not notified Veriflow, they are in breach of the security incident notification clause. Their current communication makes no reference to a potential data incident.
My decision and the reasoning behind it
The breach claim is unverified. I am making decisions as if it might be true, because if it is true and I waited for confirmation, we would have missed our 72-hour ICO notification window and failed our operational resilience obligations simultaneously. I may be responding to something that turns out to be false. I can live with that outcome. I cannot live with the alternative.

There are two separate problems here that require separate responses and I need to be careful not to conflate them. The first is the service disruption — Veriflow cannot currently process applications. The second is the potential data breach — which may or may not have occurred, and which requires a different response track.

On the service disruption: I am activating our manual review fallback process immediately. Applications received while the system is down will be queued for manual credit assessment. This is operationally painful but it is what our operational resilience plan requires. If this disruption extends beyond four hours, I will escalate to the CISO for an FCA operational resilience notification assessment.

On the potential breach: I am treating this as a possible personal data breach from this moment and starting the 72-hour ICO clock internally. The legal basis: Veriflow became aware of a credible public claim that its data processor has been breached at 07:15 this morning. "Credible" is doing work here — the claim is unverified, but it is specific, it has been corroborated by multiple people in the fintech community, and our data processor is actively experiencing a service outage consistent with an incident. That is sufficient to start our internal clock. I may be starting a process that results in no notification if the breach is not confirmed. That is the right outcome — it means we responded appropriately and the situation was less severe than feared.

Actions, in priority order

01
Activate manual application review fallback immediately — notify Customer Operations and commercial teams
Service is down. Applications need to be processed somehow. This is the operational response.
02
Notify DPO immediately — begin internal breach assessment process and start 72-hour clock
UK GDPR Art. 33 clock runs from awareness of a possible breach, not confirmed breach. We are aware.
03
Contact ClearScore AI's named security contact (not general support) and ask directly: is this a security incident? Require a response within 2 hours.
Their current communication avoids the breach question. We need a direct answer. Two hours, not four.
04
Identify and document all personal data transmitted to ClearScore AI in the last 90 days — volume, categories, applicant count
We need to know the scope of potential exposure before we can assess ICO notification requirements.
05
If no meaningful response from ClearScore AI within 2 hours, escalate to CISO for board notification and FCA operational resilience reporting assessment
At that point vendor unresponsiveness during a potential breach becomes an independent governance concern.

What I'm sitting with

The hardest judgement call here is when to treat an unverified claim as actionable. My reasoning: the 72-hour ICO clock is unforgiving, and "we were waiting for confirmation" is not a defence if confirmation comes at hour 60. The regulatory design is intentional — it forces organisations to act on possible breaches, not confirmed ones. I am following that design intent.

This incident also completes a pattern across the three scenarios. In each case, a risk that we identified in our vendor due diligence has materialised: lack of demographic monitoring capability, no pre-deployment notification for model updates, no sub-processor transparency. The vendor due diligence was accurate. The question is whether the controls we recommended were actually implemented — and in each case, the answer was partial at best. The lesson is not that due diligence failed. The lesson is that due diligence findings that don't convert into implemented controls are just a document. Arken Systems will be raising this as a programme-level finding, not just an incident-by-incident observation.
