Methodology & Design Decisions

Scenario Design

The six scenarios are arranged from more obvious attacks to more convincing ones. SC1 and SC2 still give the user a fair chance: a slightly wrong domain, an odd sender, a pressure cue that is a bit too visible. SC3 and SC4 lean more on timing, authority, and the fact that the story sounds plausible enough. SC5 and SC6 are closer to the cases people miss, especially the compromised supplier mailbox where the usual “check the sender” reflex stops being enough.

Everything sits inside one fictional organisation so the scenarios feel connected rather than randomly assembled. The attacker has done some homework. That part matters. Better BEC attacks usually do not look absurd.

Response Scoring

Each checkpoint gives four response options. These are scored across three tiers:

CORRECT The main control was identified and actually used properly. Score: 1.0
PARTIAL A sensible instinct, but it still leaves too much risk in place. Score: 0.5
INCORRECT The response would help the attacker, drop evidence, or leave the attack path open. Score: 0

The partial answers matter more than they look. In real organisations, people often do something half-right. That is usually where the real learning is.

Design Decisions
Why use scenarios instead of a policy checklist?
Because people do not usually fail in tidy policy language. They fail when something sounds urgent, when it comes from someone senior, or when slowing things down feels socially awkward.
Why include partial responses?
Because a lot of awareness tools make the wrong answers too silly. Real decisions are messier than that. A partial option helps show the gap between “not unreasonable” and “properly controlled.”
Why six scenarios in increasing difficulty?
Because BEC is not one neat category. The obvious fake-CEO email is only one version. By SC6, the usual “spot the typo” habit is not enough anymore, which is the point.
Why map to both NIST and ISO 27001?
Because the point is not only to say “this was suspicious.” It is to show how a day-to-day control failure links back to governance language that audit, risk, and compliance teams would actually recognise.
Assumptions & Limitations
01This version focuses on common BEC patterns. It does not try to cover everything, such as deepfake voice fraud or more elaborate multi-stage compromise.
02Feedback is pre-written for each response. In a real internal tool, the language and routing would need to reflect the organisation’s own procedures more closely.
03This exercise is mainly about procedural controls. It is not a technical review of DMARC, DKIM, SPF, secure email gateways, or mailbox configuration.
04It is written more for governance, finance, and operational audiences than for blue-team or email-security specialists. That was deliberate.
Governance Note

This is a portfolio exercise, but the scoring logic still needs to be visible. Security awareness tools often present a polished score and some clean framework references and leave it at that. That can look more solid than it is.

Framework refs: NIST CSF PR.AT-1, PR.AC-1, PR.AC-4, DE.CM-7, PR.IP-9, RS.CO-2 · ISO 27001 A.6.1.2, A.7.2.2, A.9.2.1, A.15.1.2, A.16.1.2

How This Works

You will see a realistic email, then move through the attack chain one checkpoint at a time. The aim is not simply to notice that something feels off. It is to choose the control response that would actually reduce the chance of loss in a real organisation. Some of the wrong answers are deliberately not ridiculous.

Decision

The controls highlighted in this assessment — supplier verification, MFA, payment approval workflows, and staff awareness — all reduce the chance of a successful BEC attack. They do not remove it completely. Social engineering still relies on people making decisions under pressure, and people will sometimes get those decisions wrong. The governance answer is not to pretend the risk disappears. It is to acknowledge the remaining risk clearly, document the compensating controls, and keep reviewing whether they are still good enough as attack patterns change.
Review trigger: any successful or near-miss BEC attempt · quarterly control effectiveness review · material change to payment processes or supplier relationships
