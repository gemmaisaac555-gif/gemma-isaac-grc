Methodology & Design Decisions

Scenario Design

Six scenarios ordered by sophistication. SC1–2 have detectable spoofing indicators (wrong domain, unusual sender). SC3–4 rely on social engineering pressure rather than technical deception. SC5–6 represent attacks that bypass standard technical controls: credential harvesting via IT impersonation, and supply chain compromise via a legitimately-held supplier domain.

All scenarios are grounded in Arken Systems as a consistent fictional organisation. Attackers conduct reconnaissance — the simulator reflects this by embedding genuine-seeming references (purchase order numbers, employee names, supplier relationships) in each attack email.

Response Scoring

Each checkpoint offers four responses rated across three outcome tiers:

CORRECT The primary control was identified and correctly applied. Score: 1.0
PARTIAL A reasonable response that addresses part of the risk but misses the primary control. Score: 0.5
INCORRECT The response enables the attack, destroys evidence, or compounds the failure. Score: 0

Partial responses are intentionally included. Most BEC incidents involve people who did something right but missed the critical step — verifying the domain but not calling to confirm, or forwarding for a second opinion but not reporting formally. The distinction between correct and partial is where the most useful learning happens.

Design Decisions
Why scenario-based rather than a policy checklist?
BEC attacks succeed not because organisations lack policies, but because those policies fail under social engineering pressure. A simulator forces control application in context — under urgency, authority bias, and plausible cover stories. The failure modes are more instructive than the correct answers.
Why include partial responses?
Many awareness tools present obvious wrong answers alongside an obvious right one. Real decisions are messier. Partial responses surface the gap between a reasonable instinct and the correct procedural control, without making the exercise feel unrealistic.
Why six scenarios in progressive order?
CEO fraud (SC1) is well-known. Supply chain compromise via a genuine domain (SC6) bypasses most technical controls people are trained to check. The cumulative gap report reveals patterns across scenarios that a single exercise cannot show.
Why map to both NIST and ISO 27001?
Arken’s regulatory context includes both frameworks. The gap report is written for a governance audience, speaking to framework obligations rather than security hygiene alone.
Assumptions & Limitations
01Covers common BEC attack patterns. Hybrid attacks, deepfake voice fraud, and multi-stage attacks are outside scope of this version.
02Control feedback is pre-authored per response. A production tool would generate feedback informed by the organisation’s actual policy documents and control library.
03Identifies control gaps at the procedural level. Does not assess technical email security controls (DMARC, DKIM, SPF) which require separate technical review.
04Designed for governance and finance audiences. A version targeting technical teams would require different scenario framing and control vocabulary.
Governance Note

This tool is a portfolio thinking exercise. The scenario design, response scoring, and gap report logic are documented here to demonstrate that security awareness tooling should be transparent about its methodology — including what it measures, what it does not measure, and the assumptions embedded in how responses are assessed.

How This Works

Each scenario presents a real email. You step through the attack chain, responding to control checkpoints as if you were the employee or manager receiving the message. At the end, you receive a control gap report mapped to NIST and ISO 27001.
