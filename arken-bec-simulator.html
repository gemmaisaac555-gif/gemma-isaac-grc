Methodology & Design Decisions

Scenario Design

The six scenarios are arranged from more obvious attacks to more convincing ones. SC1 and SC2 include clues like a wrong domain or an unusual sender. SC3 and SC4 lean more heavily on pressure, timing, and authority. SC5 and SC6 are closer to the attacks people often miss: fake IT support and compromise of a real supplier mailbox.

Everything is grounded in one fictional organisation so the situations feel coherent rather than random. The attacker has clearly done some homework. That is deliberate. Better BEC attacks do not rely on nonsense emails. They use familiar names, real suppliers, real processes, and just enough truth to feel plausible.

Response Scoring

Each checkpoint gives four response options. These are scored across three tiers:

CORRECT The key control was identified and used properly. Score: 1.0
PARTIAL A sensible instinct, but it misses the main control or leaves too much risk in place. Score: 0.5
INCORRECT The response would help the attacker, remove evidence, or fail to interrupt the attack. Score: 0

The partial answers matter. Real-world mistakes often come from people doing something that sounds reasonable but is not quite enough. That gap between “not terrible” and “actually safe” is where most of the learning sits.

Design Decisions
Why use scenarios instead of a policy checklist?
Because people rarely fail in calm, neat policy language. They fail under urgency, authority pressure, and plausible stories. Scenario-based exercises show whether a control still holds when someone feels rushed, embarrassed, or afraid of slowing the business down.
Why include partial responses?
Because many awareness tools make the wrong answers too silly. Real decisions are usually messier than that. A partial option lets the exercise show the difference between a decent instinct and a properly controlled process.
Why six scenarios in increasing difficulty?
Because BEC is not one thing. The obvious fake-CEO email is only one variant. By the time you reach the compromised supplier mailbox in SC6, the usual “check the sender domain” habit is no longer enough. That progression makes the final gap report more meaningful.
Why map to both NIST and ISO 27001?
Because the point is not just “spot the scam.” It is to show how everyday control failures connect back to governance expectations and framework language that risk, audit, and compliance teams actually use.
Assumptions & Limitations
01This version focuses on common BEC patterns. It does not cover everything, such as deepfake voice fraud or more complex multi-stage attacks.
02Feedback is pre-written for each response. In a production tool, this would ideally reflect the organisation’s own procedures, language, and control library.
03This exercise highlights procedural control gaps. It is not a technical assessment of email security settings such as DMARC, DKIM, or SPF.
04It is written mainly for governance, finance, and operational audiences. A version for technical teams would use different framing and different language.
Governance Note

This tool is a portfolio exercise, but the logic behind it is still meant to be visible. Security awareness tools should be able to explain what they measure, what they do not measure, and what assumptions sit behind the scoring. Otherwise they risk looking more rigorous than they really are.

How This Works

You will see a realistic email, then move through the attack chain one checkpoint at a time. The aim is not just to spot that something feels off. It is to choose the control response that would actually stop the attack in a real organisation. At the end, you get a gap report mapped to NIST and ISO 27001.

Decision

The controls highlighted in this assessment — supplier verification, MFA, payment approval workflows, and staff awareness — all reduce the chance of a successful BEC attack. They do not remove it completely. Social engineering still relies on people making decisions under pressure, and people will sometimes get those decisions wrong. The governance answer is not to pretend the risk disappears. It is to acknowledge the remaining risk clearly, document the compensating controls, and keep reviewing whether they are still good enough as attack patterns change.
Review trigger: any successful or near-miss BEC attempt · quarterly control effectiveness review · material change to payment processes or supplier relationships
